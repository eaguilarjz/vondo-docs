# Reports

The Reports page is where the budget stops being a chore and starts being interesting.

It's four charts, refreshed live as you record transactions, that answer the questions you've been quietly wondering for years:

- "Am I actually saving more than I spend?"
- "Where does most of my money go each month?"
- "Who am I giving the most money to?"
- "Are things getting better or worse?"

Open **Reports** from the navigation. Pick a month with the prev/next buttons at the top. The four reports below update for that month.

---

## What you'll see

| Report | Type | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| **12-month income vs. expenses** | Grouped bar chart | Each month's income (green) and expenses (red) side by side, going back 12 months |
| **12-month net savings trend** | Line chart | Income minus expenses for each of the last 12 months — the line you want to see going up |
| **Expenses by category** | Donut chart | Top 8 spending categories for the selected month, with everything else rolled into "Uncategorized" |
| **Top payees** | Horizontal bar chart | Where your money actually went this month, ranked |

All four play well with light and dark mode, and stack vertically on mobile so you can read them on a phone.

---

## 12-month income vs. expenses

This is the bird's-eye view. Each month gets a pair of bars:

- **Income** (green) — total inflows for the month
- **Expenses** (red) — total outflows for the month

When green is taller than red, you saved money that month. When red is taller, you spent more than you earned. Over a year, the pattern tells a story.

> **What to look for:**
> - Two months in a row where red is taller than green? Check what changed and address it. (One month is noise. Two is a trend.)
> - One month where expenses spiked? Probably a known thing — taxes, a big trip, holidays. Use it as a reminder to budget for the next time.
> - The "is my income consistent?" question gets answered visually here. If you're a freelancer, this chart is gold.

X-axis labels show "Month Year" — the year is omitted for the current year, just to reduce clutter. Hover any bar for the exact amount.

---

## 12-month net savings trend

Same data as the bar chart, but presented as a single line: **net savings each month** (income − expenses).

Above zero = you saved. Below zero = you spent more than you earned. The trend matters more than any single point.

This chart is the cleanest way to answer "am I building up over time?" Six months of bars all together can look noisy. The line cuts through it.

> **For example:** If your net savings line wanders between +$200 and -$150 month to month, you're roughly breaking even, with normal variance. If the line is consistently above zero and trending up, you're building. If it's consistently below zero, you're sliding — time to plan an intervention. The chart doesn't moralize; it just shows you the shape.

---

## Expenses by category

A donut chart of where your money went this month, broken down by category.

The top 8 categories get their own slices. Everything else collapses into a smaller bucket so the chart doesn't turn into a confetti pie.

Hover any slice for the exact amount and percentage. The legend on the side lists each one.

Splits are correctly attributed: a $87 grocery run that's split $54/$33 between Groceries and Household shows up as $54 in one slice and $33 in another. Transfers don't appear (they're not expenses). Opening balance transactions are excluded.

> **What to look for:**
> - Is the biggest slice what you'd expect? If "Subscriptions" is bigger than "Groceries," that's worth a second look.
> - Are there slices you don't recognize? "Misc" or "Uncategorized" growing past 5% is a sign you've got transactions that need a real category.
> - Compare to last month using the prev/next buttons at the top. The shape of the donut should be roughly stable. Big shifts have a story.

---

## Top payees

A horizontal bar chart ranking the payees you spent the most with this month.

This is the chart that produces those "wait, *that* much at Amazon??" moments. It's a useful reality check.

Income transactions are excluded — this is about where your money went, not where it came from.

> **For example:** Whole Foods is your #1 payee at $487. Trader Joe's is #4 at $108. Total grocery spending across both is $595, which is $95 over your $500 grocery budget. The chart didn't budget for you, but it did show you the truth — fast.

---

## Time controls

The prev/next buttons at the top of the page navigate the **selected month** for the category breakdown and top payees. The 12-month charts at the top are always rolling, ending on whatever month you've selected.

You can navigate as far back as you have data. **Next** is disabled when you reach the current month — savr won't let you peek at a month that hasn't happened.

---

## Reports vs. Budget

Quick distinction:

| Page | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Budget** | "Where am I going to spend my money this month?" — forward-looking |
| **Reports** | "Where did I actually spend my money?" — backward-looking |

You'll use Budget more often (probably daily). You'll use Reports less often but get more from it (probably weekly or monthly). They feed each other: this month's reports inform next month's budget targets.

---

## Tips

- **Don't open Reports during the first few days of a new month.** A two-day-old month is a sample of two days. Wait a week before drawing conclusions.
- **The "trend" line is more useful than any single month's number.** Variance is normal. Direction is meaningful.
- **Use the donut to clean up your categories.** If one slice is "Misc" with 30% of your spending, your category structure isn't doing its job. Add real categories and reassign.
- **Take a screenshot at the end of each month.** It's surprisingly motivating to look back at six months of these charts and see your trend line going up.
- **Don't moralize.** Reports show you what is, not what should be. Use them as data, not judgment.
